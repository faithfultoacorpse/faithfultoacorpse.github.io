Just quit reading this and do something for me: take a look at your life. What are you proud of? What actions do you identify with? Perhaps your degree, your job, the books you read or the art you make? When you're meeting new people, and you're nervous about making a good impression, what do you wish you could naturally bring into the conversation to impress them? Those things, is it really you doing all of them? Of course you did, someone did, and they had your body, so who else could it have been?

I've got experiences like that too, I've got accomplishments. I haven't changed the world per say, but my family says I've made them proud. By no means am I some form of inhuman prodigy, nor was I groomed for success by engineer parents (Not that there's anything wrong with you are an inhuman prodigy or if you were groomed for success by engineer parents). Allegedly, I am "living the dream." Or whatever. But it doesn't really feel like *my* dream, and it doesn't really feel like *I* did it. Looking back at the events that brought me to my goals is like watching a Bulgarian art-house film: it makes no sense. Without being hyperbolic, an entity possessed me when I was 14. No, really, that's what it felt like in the moment, that's what it looks like in hindsight, and that's what I'm trying to get you to see (whether this has happened to you or not)!

The Possession

None of my family had been to college, I didn't have many close connections who had been to college, and yet with zero guidance for what to do to "become somebody," my body was compelled to do various things that would increase my odds of succeeding. Not in any sort of methodical, rational sense. I didn't do research on how to get into college or anything silly like that, one day I woke up and decided I would be the captain of the tennis team, and enrolled in as many AP classes as possible, and I entered student government, and I started volunteering, and I got mentors. And I didn't do any of that. To wit, I was piloted by a (hopefully) divine force, completely against my control. It paid off quite well, materially, but the actions which transformed me into a wunderkind - relative to my younger, more mischevious self - were not the sorts of things that the same mischevious self would have done; the side-effects of that drive required sacrifices that the mischevious one would not have taken.

Deep down, I'm a good old boy. I love my grandma, and I sneak fingers of cake batter when she's baking, and I stay up late to eat the snacks I'm not supposed to have, and I help my mom garden, and I ride my bike around the neighborhood with all the other good old boys. And I gave it all away.

Perhaps, you're not like me: you're decisive, have always been driven, knew what you wanted to do, executed a visible agency to do so, and so on. In that case, I do not think you'll understand the things I'm talking about. Apparently Wittgenstein said a similar thing (I will not pretend to have read the book yet, thank you eggreport)

This book will perhaps only be understood by those who have themselves already thought the thoughts which are expressed in it--or similar thoughts. It is therefore not a text-book. its object would be attained if there were one person who read it with understanding and to whom it afforded pleasure.
- Wittgenstein, Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus

Unlike one of the earlier referenced persons, I'm not decisive or the other things; I kind of like money inasmuch as I can spend it, but I don't really have much I'd like to spend it on anyways; I enjoy learning but I value humility more than knowledge, and I wouldn't like to be the kind of person who needs to appear smart to the people around me; most of all, I'm rather lazy and weak willed! All of this begs the question: what was it all for? What did the other me intend to manifest through my possession?

Hold on, I'm getting ahead of myself. The correct first question is, am I just schizophrenic?

I'm probably not crazy

It's odd, actually, I've stumbled upon a number of similar sentiments by chance in the past few weeks, and while writing this I've recollected a few more. You read the Borges quote in the beginning (By the way, read the whole poem for god's sake, it's like 5 lines). I heard it mentioned while listening to Dwarkesh Patel's interview with Gwern Branwen for the third or fourth time. It's comforting knowing that certifiably, this phenomenon has not just happened to me. Importantly, Borges doesn't beat around the bush, it is almost exactly the same feeling I have. But It's not just Borges either, read this:

Every man has a twofold life: On one side is his personal life, which is free in proportion as its interests are abstract; the other is life as an element, as one bee in the swarm; and here a man has no chance of disregarding the laws impose on him. The higher a man stands on the social ladder, the more men he is connected with, the greater the influence he exerts over others--the more evident is the predestined and unavoidable necessity of his every action. The king is the slave of history. In the events of history, so-called great men are merely tags that supply a name to the event, and have quite as little connection with the event itself as the tag. Every one of their actions, though apparently performed by their own free will, is, in its historical significance, out of the scope of volition, and is correlated with the whole trend of history; and is, consequently, preordained from all eternity.
- Leo Tolstoy, War and Peace

The primary difference between my ramblings and Tolstoy's is that one need not be a "great man of history" to fall victim to such forces. I've got more, look!

when it is truly time,
and if you have been chosen,
it will do it by
itself and it will keep on doing it
until you die or it dies in you.

there is no other way.

and there never was.
- Charles Bukowski, so you want to be a writer?

Extrapolate beyond Bukowski's reference to writing: *nothing* is worth doing unless you are "chosen". Moreso, a separate doer will overtake you until either you -- the watcher -- die, or the doer dies. Then, Bukowski marks three, quod erat demonstrandum!

If that's not enough, look at every buddhist ever. One of the common introductions of how to meditate involves separating the thinker and the watcher: one is supposed to embody the watcher by observing as thoughts come and go without judgement. I'll omit any quotes, but Ram Dass echoed exactly such a sentiment, and the act of embodying the watcher is what separated him from his doer's role in Cambridge... that, and maybe all of the acid.

So Borges, Tolstoy, and Bukowski made three, every buddhist ever makes, like, three plus 400 million? I mean, surely that's quod erat demonstratum that this isn't totally out there.

There are two jungle cats inside of you

Merab Abramishvili - Leopard and Black Panther

My point is, you're at least two jungle cats -- I mean, people. One of you controlled your body when you were an amalgamation of motor reflexes and sensory data, but as the stimulus lost its novelty a second you emerged and begins acting upon the world. This means I lied earlier, I was being hyperbolic when I said I was possessed, surely we have the ability to balance between these two cats -- I mean, people. Any sacrifices which were made by my other were implicitly agreed to by me. I think that's just how this life works, people get sucked into their jobs, their hobbies, their personas *all the time*; and yet, people get sucked oneshot by passivity all the time as well.

It's a blessing to be possessed, to feel so compelled by something that you can pour yourself entirely into it. But it's a tragedy to sacrifice the rest that this life offers in pursuit of it. As I mentioned before, I'm really not some sort of prodigal child, and neither was my other. It took an immense amount of effort and time to achieve those goals. At points, my own family seemed to me strangers, or perhaps vice-versa; family cannot be bought, conjured by reading, or derived from any set of equations, losing family due to self-serving actions is a terrible loss. This whole post started as a warning to the currently possessed: do not sacrifice bonds with whoever you choose to call family for your goals.

The pendulum swings. It's why the other other me, the first one, the other cat, the-- It's why that one is writing this now. I have felt another loss, the possessor achieved their more immediate goals, and the one who was watching was startled awake from the vacuum that still lingered. This life is a fine balance between these two cats. It's basically democracy. Or maybe it's more like the 4-year presidential cycle in the US. Whatever!

I'm not entirely sure, but I think that identifying the ridge, the narrow path so to speak, which pleases both cats is what leads one to a greater sense of fulfillment. That's what I'm attempting to do now.

But, I don't really know. I'm still young, naive, and just trying to figure out what has happened to me in the past 7 years. And I don’t know which of us wrote this.
